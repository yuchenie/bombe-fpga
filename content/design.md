The Enigma machine was an encryption technology that Nazi Germany used to prevent the decryption of their communications throughout WWII. Critical information was able to be sent by any means, and intercepted by any spy—and the Enigma encryption prevented any critical intelligence from being compromised. A group of mathematicians at Bletchley Park—of which the most well-known and influential was Alan Turing—discovered key weaknesses in the encryption technique and developed a decryption operation using a combination of electromechanical devices and human resources. The operation was shrouded in secrecy to prevent Nazi forces from discovering their own weakness, ultimately allowing the Allies to receive critical military information of such importance that many argue it transformed the landscape of war.

It is a misnomer to call the Bombe machine the culmination of the Bletchley Park operations. In reality, the Bombe machine was one step of many in decoding a message. Similarly, our final project encapsulates the entire process carried out by the Bletchley Park operations. More context on the Enigma machine and the Bletchley Park decryption operations are detailed in the [pages]. The steps to decryption can be summarized:

1. Crib detection. Using the knowledge that a certain piece of text is encoded within the message, a crib sweeper algorithm filters possible positions at which the known text (the “crib”) might be in the entire message. This produces a series of text windows that serve as potential ciphers of the crib.
2. Loop detection. The Bombe algorithm operated on a “loop” in the crib-cipher pair. For each crib-cipher pair produced by the crib detection, a loop detection algorithm would find letter pairs that connect each letter in the loop to two other letters at some index in the crib-cipher pair.
3. Bombe machine. Each letter pair in the loop corresponds to a letter index, and each letter index corresponds to a unique transformation executed by the Enigma machine. The transformation at an offset is generated as the equivalent circuit. The relationship between the transformations can be inferred from the offset between the indices. The Bombe machine brute-forces the equivalent circuits for each of the 26^3 possible initial conditions. It uses relationships between the letters in the loop to produce a list of initial conditions for which the letters in the loop could possibly have been generated.
4. Plugboard. 
5. Enigma machine. With the result of the plugboard deduction algorithm, the bidirectionality of the Enigma machine is used to determine if the initial conditions and plugboard will map the cipher to the crib. This step eliminates every false stop.
6. Result interpretation. The algorithm produces a valid initial condition for the first index of the crib-cipher match. Using the offset into the message that generated the cipher-crib pair, the initial condition for the entire message is backed out. The plugboard remains as deduced in the fourth step.

The algorithms are thoughtfully divided between the FPGA and HPS to optimize the distribution of computational complexity and the volume of data transfer.

FPGA. The brute-force Bombe algorithm is the most computationally heavy aspect of the process. This is the central algorithm executed on the FPGA. Notably, we determined in Python simulation that each cipher-crib pair passed through the Bombe machine produces thousands of valid results—it would be unreasonable for the FPGA processing to pass every valid result to the HPS for further checking. Therefore, the remainder of the process to eliminate false stops must also be executed on the FPGA: The plugboard deduction and the enigma machine comparison. 

HPS. Communication between the FPGA and HPS for each stop of the Bombe machine is unreasonable, because the stops would be computed within milliseconds of each other. However, a period up to 1.6 seconds elapses to execute the entire brute-force Bombe algorithm, plugboard deduction, and Enigma validation stages of a single cipher-crib pair. This makes communication between the FPGA and HPS between the evaluation of different cipher-crib pairs reasonable. Therefore, computation that is executed once for each cipher-crib pair—specficially, the loop-detection algorithm—is performed on the HPS and passed to the FPGA. Other top-level processes, such as the crib detector and the user interface, are also executed on the HPS.

Our final project is a combination of internally designed algorithms and the utilization of Bletchley Park algorithms. Our original algorithms execute loop detection of cipher-crib pairs and plugboard deduction. We replicate the algorithms of the Enigma machine and the Bombe machine, and use Bletchley Park strategies to perform crib sweeping. The Enigma machine patent (US1905593) expired in 1949 and can be legally recreated in any form. The Bombe machine was not patented. All programs produced are completely original.
